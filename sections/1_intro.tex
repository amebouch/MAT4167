\section{Introduction}
\begin{center}
\textbf{Preliminary Notions}
\end{center}
\medskip
\medskip 
{\color{gray}\hrule}
\medskip
The fundamental theorem of arithmetic states that every integer greater than 1 can be uniquely factorized into prime numbers. 
This is a very useful and important theorem in number theory. 
However, when one passes to number field $K$ and their ring of integers $\mathcal{O}_K$, this property no longer holds for elements. 
Unique factorization into irreducible may even fail. 

\medskip 

One might consider ideals instead of elements. A result in algebraic number theory states that every non zero ideal in $\mathcal{O}_K$, factors uniquely into prime ideals. 
That brings us to think how prime numbers $p \in \mathbb{Z}$ decompose in the ring $\mathcal{O}_K$.

\medskip 

In this paper, we will explore how the decomposition of a prime $p$ in $\mathcal{O}_K$ is determined by the factorization of a defining polynomial modulo $p$.

\medskip
